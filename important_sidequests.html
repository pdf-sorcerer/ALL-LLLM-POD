ALL-LLM Pod looks like a character-chat app, but most of the engineering work happened underneath the surface. This page catalogs the significant side systems that made the main apps possible: ingestion tools, transcript repair workflows, remote operations consoles, speaker attribution systems, and runtime utilities built to keep the corpus alive as the project grew.

Operations Console
Clownfish V2
A remote control plane for ingestion, transcription, diarization, transcript review, and corpus maintenance.
+

As the corpus expanded from individual episodes to hundreds of hours of audio — and eventually to entirely new speakers outside the original All-In cast — maintaining the dataset became increasingly operationally expensive.

Early transcript generation workflows were locked to the Windows workstation, either locally or through Tailscale. Adding new content sources often meant creating one-off scripts to download, process, and integrate material into the pipeline. This worked initially, but became difficult to scale as the project grew.

To solve this, I built Clownfish V2: a remote operations layer sitting on top of the core AI pipeline.

  • Download and enqueue content from Libsyn, YouTube, local files, or arbitrary URLs.
  • Execute WhisperX transcription pipelines against queued content.
  • Run NeMo diarization and generate speaker-attributed transcripts using stable speaker IDs.
  • Fuse ASR and diarization outputs into reviewable transcript artifacts.
  • Launch Brody human-in-the-loop review workflows to inspect transcripts, repair attribution errors, and resolve edge cases.
  • Export finalized transcripts for downstream training, retrieval, and persona-generation workflows.
  • Monitor pipeline execution, queue state, logs, and corpus health from a single interface.

Running on the Windows orchestration server and accessible from iPhone, Mac, or any browser, Clownfish V2 became the operational control plane for the corpus.

Transcript QA
Brody
A lexical HITL review surface for inspecting fused transcripts, finding broken speaker turns, and applying quality fixes.
+

Brody exists because transcript quality problems are rarely solved by one better model. The failures are usually local: a dropped interjection, a split speaker turn, an invented boundary, a short utterance assigned to the wrong person, or a segment that looks fine until it is read in context.

The tool turns transcript repair into an inspection workflow instead of a pile of scripts. It gives the corpus a review layer where suspicious turns can be found, checked, corrected, and exported back into the training and retrieval datasets.

input audio -> ASR transcript -> diarized speaker IDs -> fused transcript -> Brody lexical review -> corrected training / retrieval artifact

Audio Forensics
Ghost Audio Investigation
A transcript reliability investigation into speech that appeared in one source, vanished in another, or existed in audio but failed ASR.
+

The ghost audio work began as a simple discrepancy: the same episode could produce different transcript content depending on the source, processing path, and ASR behavior. Some words existed in YouTube transcripts but not in Libsyn-derived transcripts. Other short utterances had measurable audio energy but disappeared from both outputs.

That turned transcript generation into a forensic problem. The lesson was not just that ASR can be wrong. It was that transcript systems can fail silently, and those silent omissions can change the meaning of a conversation while leaving the surrounding transcript looking clean.

Speaker Attribution
Classifier Scaling
The progression from host-only classification to guest-aware speaker attribution with human review.
+

The first classifier solved the narrow problem: identify the four recurring hosts. That worked until the dataset started containing more guests, more short interjections, more cross-talk, and more cases where the closest host was not the correct speaker.

Scaling speaker attribution required a different framing. The system needed to preserve unknowns, support guest speakers, propagate identity across stable diarization IDs, and keep a human review loop close to the data.

V1: host-only classifier V2: guest-aware labeling + Clownfish review V3: fused transcript workflow + Brody inspection

Runtime Support
Freshness & Memory Retrieval
Support systems that let personas answer from both long-term corpus memory and current-event context.
+

The personas needed more than style imitation. They needed access to prior positions, recurring arguments, dated claims, and fresh context when a question depended on current events.

This led to memory retrieval, topic routing, online freshness checks, and prompt assembly logic that could decide when to rely on corpus memory, when to search for newer context, and how to inject the result into the persona runtime without overwhelming the answer.

Voice Runtime
TTS & Streaming Playback
The voice layer that turned generated persona text into playable audio while managing startup and latency tradeoffs.
+

Voice generation was a separate runtime problem from text generation. The app needed stable reference voices, predictable startup behavior, streaming playback, and enough latency control that the system felt conversational instead of batch-generated.

The resulting voice path became another side system: reference preparation, model warmup, websocket delivery, audio playback, and instrumentation around time-to-first-audio and completion time.
