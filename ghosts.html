Ghost Audio Investigation

Ghost Audio

Late in the project, I switched from a hodgepodge of YouTube podcast audio to full episode masters from Libsyn. The assumption seemed obvious: higher-quality source audio should produce higher-quality transcripts. Instead, I found older transcripts generated from objectively worse YouTube audio sometimes contained dialogue missing entirely from the cleaner Libsyn rebuilds. I coined this phenomenon Ghost Audio because root-causing it felt like a paranormal investigation from the 1990s TV show Sightings: time-consuming, frustrating, and rarely ending with a clean explanation.

The early tip-off came from Episode 136. Friedberg’s quiet “oh my god” appeared in an older transcript but vanished entirely from the Libsyn rebuild. Brad Gerstner said nearly the exact same phrase moments later — also missing. Acoustic inspection confirmed Friedberg’s utterance physically existed: VAD-positive, RMS energy present, recoverable pitch after spectral subtraction, and faint activation visible in the cross-attention map. The model was not “deaf” to the audio. The decoder perceived the utterance and still failed to emit tokens for it.

The breakthrough came from inspecting beam search behavior directly, which required temporarily dropping WhisperX’s bolted-on CTranslate2 backend in favor of reference Whisper running through PyTorch so decoder internals could be observed directly. Friedberg’s “oh my god” existed inside the decoder’s probability space, but scored too weakly relative to louder surrounding speech. The decoder repeatedly pruned the quieter path in favor of Friedberg’s follow-up line (“He’s not even here”). Small decoding changes — beam width, VAD chunking, timestamp conditioning, segmentation density, and temperature — produced materially different conversational reconstructions from the same audio. After a long detour tuning parameters, the answer turned out to be structural rather than parametric: replacing the pipeline itself. I moved from WhisperX to a hybrid stack of pyannote VAD → reference Whisper → wav2vec2 forced alignment (“pww”), which recovered Friedberg-class ghosts reliably by creating denser segmentation boundaries around quiet speech. Gerstner-class ghosts remained unrecoverable because the decoder never meaningfully surfaced the utterance in the first place.

That investigation fundamentally changed how I thought about transcript systems. You often hear that AI systems and LLMs are “probability machines,” but it’s one thing hearing that phrase and another tracing the reconstruction process closely enough to internalize what it actually means. Transcript pipelines were not producing deterministic ground truth; they were producing probabilistic reconstructions with failure modes that often looked superficially correct. The final architecture shifted away from treating ASR output as unquestioned truth and toward treating transcripts as observable infrastructure requiring disagreement analysis, forensic review, repair tooling, and constrained Human-in-the-Loop workflows. The system now flags its own failures instead of pretending they aren’t there.

The unsettling realization was that transcript pipelines do not steadily converge toward objective truth as models improve. Architectural changes recover some speech, erase other speech, and occasionally create entirely new recoverable utterances that were previously invisible. The system was not uncovering a fixed transcript hidden inside the audio. It was reconstructing one probabilistic interpretation of conversational reality.
