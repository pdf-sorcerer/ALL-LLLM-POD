Ghosts in the Audio

I automated the All-In Podcast — its four hosts are now persona chatbots I can interact with in my app, ALL-LLM Pod. But the transcripts that power them are more interesting than the chatbots. They're the foundation of the training data, created by an AI pipeline that transcribes audio into words and classifies speech into speaker_ids. And AI struggles with short-form, conversational audio like All-In — it's much more susceptible to mistakes that degrade the "ground-truth" quality of the transcript.

A couple failure modes:

  • Speaker misclassification — speech attributed to the wrong person passes into that person's training data as text. Teaches the wrong model things it never said; denies the rightful model its own content.
  • Ghost audio — speech that is never transcribed, creating gaps in the conversation that the models then learn patterns from.

The two videos from Empire Strikes Back below are staged to show both failure modes — just imagine that decades-old text transcripts were ruled cost-ineffective and replaced by generative AI streaming subtitles in real time.

Almost everyone spots the Empire errors because we all know the source material. Across 358 episodes of All-In, the same failure modes are running — just invisibly. The chatbot personas that emerge from the training data don't sound wrong. They respond like the hosts, and potentially know things the hosts never said.

The Scene: All-In Podcast Episode 136

Ghost audio was first witnessed in the opening of Episode 136 of All-In. The episode opens without Jcal, the hosts' usual moderator. Friedberg raises the question of how to open the show — Chamath responds by mimicking Jcal's speech mannerisms in an unflattering way (he roasts Jcal). Everyone laughs.

Friedberg catches his breath, muttering "oh my god" — which goes untranscribed. This is Friedberg's ghost (aka ghost #1). Moments later, Brad Gerstner says exactly the same thing: "oh my god." Also untranscribed. This is Gerstner's ghost (aka ghost #2).

Audio Files

Friedberg's ghost (ghost #1) had been successfully transcribed weeks earlier from a low-quality YouTube file — one of 500+ clips, episodes, and shorts ripped from various All-In channels. The newer transcript was created from a file downloaded via Libsyn, which I had switched to for a canonical episode record. Paradoxically, the Libsyn file was higher quality but ended up omitting content the lower-quality YouTube rip had captured. Why? File comparison

Forensic Analysis

To answer that, I walked the two files through the transcription pipeline stage by stage and compared what each one did with the ghost. Every stage is a place the ghost could have been lost — and the YouTube file's ability to capture it, despite being the worse recording, meant the failure wasn't about audio quality. It was about how the pipeline handled what it heard.

  1. Voice Activity Detection — did the pipeline register speech at the ghost's timestamp in each file?
  2. Spectral Subtraction — is Friedberg's signal actually in the Libsyn audio, buried under Chamath?
  3. Model Substitution — does swapping Whisper versions change the outcome?
  4. Beam Search Autopsy — inside the decoder, why did one candidate sequence win over the other?

The pipeline heard Friedberg in both files. It only transcribed him in one.

1 — Voice Activity Detection (VAD)

Question: 'Can AI "hear" Friedberg's ghost segment? I used external Silero VAD measures how confident the model is that someone is speaking. Scores are in probability of speech: high means active speech, low means silence, 0.50 is the threshold.

Silero VAD is stateful, meaning Its score is influenced by what it has just finished hearing. Four separate runs were made from T=20 to T=30, each produced different curves. Mode 3 (cold start) is what I'll use going forward, because it shows the YouTube file having a voice activity dip around 20.4s; which is close to Friedberg's ghost segment. At the time I figured WhisperX created a speaker boundry there; but jokes on me - the entire segment 0-3xs is one segment.

The YouTube file shows discrete voice boundaries within [21.4–21.8], [21.85–22.4], [22.4–…]. The Libsyn file treats everything from t=21.4s onward as one continuous six-second segment. No boundaries, no dips below threshold. The early hypothesis was that Friedberg's ghost was being absorbed into a larger, more crowded audio region of his own voice where it was out-competed.

Follow-up work revealed that WhisperX treated t=1 to t=31 as one segment.

2 — Spectral Subtraction

WhisperX uses a transformer attention architecture, meaning dominant speech will draw more attention and quit utterances like Friedberg and Gerstner's ghosts will be out-competed. As it turns out, Transfoerms did pay attention to ghost semgnet - just not much


So, i thought: "why map the domiant audio and and calibrate against it, and take a second pass to pickup the residual?"

A two-pass approach: use the preceding window (Chamath) as a noise profile, subtract it from the ghost window, look for residual signal. Flickers of pitch activity — previously hidden — became visible in the F0 graph. The signal is there. The pipeline was dropping something real.

4 — The Beam Search Autopsy

[placeholder: explain beam search]

The first question: did the model even attend to the ghost segment? The attention distribution graph plots where Whisper focused during transcription. Each spike is the model weighting a moment in the audio; the three markers at t=21–22s are the ground truth positions of "Oh," "my," and "god."

The ghost has attention — just small, dwarfed by the surrounding spikes. The model looked at Friedberg's utterance and kept moving.

attention graph

The audio was physically present (VAD confirmed it), the model processed it (attention confirmed it), and it still didn't make it into the transcript. Something downstream of perception dropped it. That something was beam search.

Beam search holds multiple candidate sequences in parallel and scores them cumulatively; the highest-scoring sequence wins. Friedberg's "Oh my God" was quiet and hesitant. Its opening token — "Oh" — scored low enough that beam search pruned the entire path in favor of Friedberg's subsequent, more dominant speech: "He's not even here. That's awful" around t=23s.

This also ruled out anti-hallucination filtering. Whisper was trained on YouTube, where "oh my God" is common noise, and a hallucination filter would be a reasonable explanation for the drop. But the beam search data showed the model was hearing the ghost and doing nothing with it.

The failure was compound. V3 dropped the ghost only when all four conditions were present simultaneously — remove any one and the ghost reappears:

Why V2 and V3 Behave Differently

Systematic testing across 128 parameter combinations — 25 trials each — revealed that large-v2's raw decoder never recovers the ghost: zero recoveries across every configuration tested. V2 isn't more forgiving at the decoding stage. It's more forgiving at the alignment stage.

What actually happens: v2's decoder produces clean surrounding context — "the dumbest" followed by a period — which gives whisperx's forced aligner a clean boundary to work from. When wav2vec2 scans the audio for the next word after that boundary, it finds acoustic evidence for "Oh" and commits to it. The decoder didn't recover the ghost. The aligner did.

V3 is the opposite case. Its decoder has stronger acoustic awareness of the ghost — at the critical decision step after "dumbest," "Oh" scores 23.45% versus "He" at 71.68%. The model heard Friedberg. But v3's probability distribution is sharper and more peaked than v2's, meaning the gap between "He" and "Oh" is decisive for greedy decoding. The ghost path gets pruned. And because the decoder never outputs "oh my god," the aligner never goes looking for it.

This creates an asymmetry: v2 recovers the ghost deterministically because its forced aligner succeeds. V3 suppresses it deterministically because its decoder fails first — and the aligner can't recover what the decoder never proposed.

A third recovery path exists. With temperature raised to 0.2 and VAD segmentation disabled, v3's stochastic decoder crosses the "Oh"/"He" decision boundary in 60–72% of independent runs. This is mechanistic confirmation that the ghost sits at a genuine acoustic decision boundary — real but competing probability mass on both sides. Run it once greedily and the ghost disappears. Introduce sampling noise and it surfaces more often than not.

For production use, v2 + forced alignment is the stronger choice: deterministic, reproducible, no cherry-picking. The v3 temperature finding is supporting evidence — it proves the ghost voice is real speech at a genuine decision boundary — but v2 + FA is the recovery method.

Research note At one point during testing, setting no_speech_threshold=0.3, log_prob_threshold=-1.0, and batch_size=8 surfaced Friedberg's segment in the Libsyn transcript. The result did not reproduce on subsequent runs. The parameters were logged — the discrepancy remains unexplained.

The Lesson

AI is confidently incorrect, which breeds complacency in its operators. These failure modes were found through forensic review — listening to raw audio, watching video, comparing pipeline runs. The ghost's omission passed silently; discovering it required spotting a discrepancy between runs, a needle-in-a-haystack event given half a million such segments across 358 episodes. Automated pipelines succeed while dropping real speech. Systematic manual review is the only defense — and even that won't catch everything.

Misclassification — Boeing MCAS (2018)▼
Background
Boeing's MCAS relied on a single angle-of-attack sensor. When that sensor failed, MCAS repeatedly pushed the nose down. Two crashes. 346 people died.
Audio Pipeline NeMo's speaker embeddings are the single sensor: a low-signal embedding carries the same authority as a high-signal one. Early in an episode, NeMo has heard little of each speaker. When Friedberg speaks at 21 seconds, his voice is assigned to the closest available cluster — Gerstner. The misattribution is never revisited. Errors propagate downstream.
Key Difference — Consequence Boeing's failure had a body count. A mislabeled training set has a quality count — quieter, slower, easier to miss. Same mechanism: single input, high confidence, no verification loop, errors that compound.
