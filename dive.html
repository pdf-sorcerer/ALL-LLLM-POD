Ghosts in the Audio

ASR transcription technology is capable of producing high-qualiuty transcripts that are clean, structured, and look complete enough to engender complacency. They are not infallible; a couple classes error can pass silently onto the transcript and remain there unless detected manually.

1. Ghost Audio — speech was heard, but not found on the transcript. Omitted from training data.
2. Misclassification — speech assigned to the wrong speaker. Corrupts persona training data.

Both errors are common in short-form conversational audio, whcih is what All-In Podcast is. In fact, 43% of the N speaker segments fall below this threshold. Convention encourages dropping those segments wholesale. I wanted to try another way. The examples described below in 'Evidence from Transcripts' illustrate the failure mode. The architecture that produced them is the larger problem (and one worth solving).

Useful analogies of ghost audio & misclassification
Ghost Audio — Crimson Tide (film) ▼
Crimson Tide
In the 1995 film Crimson Tide, the submarine USS Alabama receives an Emergency Action Message (EAM) ordering the launch of nuclear missiles. A second EAM follows but cuts off mid-transmission when the radio fails.

The Captain (Gene Hackman) and First Officer (Denzel Washington) interpret the situation differently. The Captain assumes the prior launch order still stands; the First Officer believes the second EAM may contain a cancellation. The crew splits into opposing factions along those lines.
Audio Pipeline Friedberg's "Oh, my God" at 21.4s appeared in an earlier transcript run but was absent in subsequent runs against the Libsyn file. Attempts to reproduce the conditions that first surfaced it were unsuccessful. The signal exists in the raw audio — spectral subtraction confirms it is there — but a reliable pipeline path to recover it has not been found.

Translated to the film: the Alabama's radio didn't fail. The second EAM was received. But in the noise of the control room, no one noticed it arrive — and there is no clean way to play it back.
Key Difference — Visibility In Crimson Tide, everyone knows a second message exists and that its record is incomplete. In the audio pipeline, the ghost segment's omission passes silently. Discovering it required spotting a discrepancy between runs — a needle-in-a-haystack event given there are half a million such segments across 358 episodes. Confirming the signal is present required working around the pipeline entirely.
Misclassification — Boeing MCAS (2018) ▼
Background
The Boeing 737 MAX introduced a flight-control system called the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) to counter a pitch-up tendency created by rushed design changes made to the airplane during development.

MCAS relied on input from a single angle-of-attack (AoA) sensor, even though two AoA sensors were installed on the aircraft. When that single sensor produced a faulty reading, MCAS took control of the plane and put it into a nose-down position. Pilots fought to regain control, but roughly every ten seconds MCAS re-engaged based on the same bad sensor input. Two crashes followed. 346 people died.
Audio Pipeline NeMo's speaker embeddings are analogous to the 737 MAX's single sensor: a low-signal, low-quality embedding carries the same authority over speaker assignment as a high-signal, high-quality one.

The classifier was not designed to cross-check against a second signal or flag low confidence. Early in an episode, NeMo has heard very little of each speaker. When Friedberg speaks at 21 seconds, the model has not yet encountered a clear, unambiguous example of Brad Gerstner — so it assigns Friedberg's voice to the closest available cluster, which at that moment is Gerstner. The misattribution is never revisited. Those errors propagate silently downstream, contaminating training data.
Key Difference — Consequence Boeing's failure had a body count. A mislabeled training set has a quality count — quieter, slower, and easier to miss. The mechanism is the same: single input, high confidence, no verification loop, errors that compound downstream.

Episode 136 TitaNet Embedding Manifold

Embeddings are the digital voiceprint of a speaker in 192-dimensional vector space. We can project the first 30 seconds of embeddings onto a hypersphere plane for convenience and watch where they cluster. Each cluster develops into a distinct speaker voiceprint.

The first 30 seconds of Episode 136 show the audio conditions that create ghost segments and misclassification errors. Friedberg's "Oh, my God" at 21.4 seconds is audible but faint. Gerstner's response at 27.4 seconds is barely perceptible even with video confirmation.

Forensic Confirmation via Spectral Subtraction

When attempts to reproduce the ghost segment through pipeline tuning failed, a different question became more useful: is the signal actually present in the raw audio, independent of what the pipeline does with it?

Spectral subtraction uses the dominant speaker's audio immediately before the boundary as a noise profile, then subtracts it from the window where the ghost is expected. What remains — the residual — isolates the weaker signal. Applied to the 19.7–24.7 second window of Episode 136, the residual shows clear acoustic activity at the timestamps where Friedberg's words were recorded in the earlier transcript. The signal is there. The pipeline is dropping it.

This does not explain why the pipeline drops it, and it does not constitute a recovery. It establishes that the failure is a pipeline problem, not an absence of signal — which is a necessary precondition for any eventual fix.

UMAP Embeddings Manifold

Speaker classification introduces a second, independent failure mode. Early in an episode, NeMo has minimal information about each speaker's voice. When Friedberg speaks at 21 seconds, the model has not yet heard a clear, audible example of Brad Gerstner. The system assigned Friedberg's voice to the closest available cluster — which at the time was Brad Gerstner. Cosine similarity confirmed the segment is actually closest to Friedberg's voice.

As the episode advances, speaker embeddings mature and cluster representation shifts — however, the earlier mislabeling is never revisited. The transcript appears complete. The pipeline exits cleanly. The misattribution remains hidden.

Implications for Training Data

Every misattributed line becomes training data. Speaker attribution errors rarely surface as failures — the transcript appears correct and the pipeline produces no warnings. Incorrect labels quietly propagate into downstream datasets.

Two-pass diarization addresses part of the problem, and human-in-the-loop labeling can catch residual errors. But the deeper issue is architectural: single-pass diarization on long-form audio is biased against early segments.

The first minute of a three-hour episode is labeled with dramatically less information than the final hour. Across hundreds of episodes, that asymmetry compounds.
