Ghosts in the Audio

Ghosts Ghosts are short speech segments the transcription pipeline failed to record. Misclassifications are segments it recorded but attributed to the wrong person. Both failures are common in short-form audio — segments under five seconds. Forty-three percent of All-In utterances fall below that threshold.

Neither failure announces itself. Omissions and misattributed quotes pass into training data silently, corrupting the persona corpus. The two cases below illustrate the failure mode. The architecture that produced them is the larger problem.

Useful analogies of ghost audio & misclassification
Ghost Audio — Crimson Tide (movie) ▼
Movie: In the 1995 movie 'Crimson Tide', the USS Alabama receives an emergency action message (EAM), which is text-based, ordering a nuclear launch. A second EAM arrives immediately after — and cuts off mid-message because the ship loses its radio while under attack.

The crew spends the rest of the film talking about the missing half of the second EAM and what to do while the radio is under repair.
Audio Pipeline: Friedberg and Gerstner's ghost audio ("Oh, my God") was not captured in recent runs, but an earlier transcript run confirmed something had been there. Pivoting to the movie: this would be as if the second EAM was transmitted in full, but the submarine's radio was scanning too broad a band — and the narrow VLF signal fell through undetected.
Key Difference: Visibility. Crimson Tide, everyone knows the second message exists and it's incomplete. My pipeline? I got lucky.
Misclassification — Cold War (1983) ▼
Cold War: In 1983, a Soviet early-warning satellite detected a launch of US ICBMs. Ground radar showed nothing. Instead of retaliating, Stanislav Petrov waited, reasoning that a real first strike would involve hundreds of missiles — not the five the satellite was reporting. He was right. The satellite had misinterpreted sunlight reflecting off clouds as a launch.
Audio Pipeline: NeMo's voice classifier builds speaker profiles through embeddings, which mature in accuracy as the episode progresses.

When Friedberg spoke at 21 seconds, the model had paper-thin data to make an assignment. It assigned Friedberg's voice to the closest available centroid and moved on. Confidently. The transcript shows "Gerstner", other times it shows "Chamath." It never shows "Friedberg." No flag, no warning, no asterisk.
Key Difference: Petrov had an instinct to distrust a confident system — and we're alive today because of it.

The classifier can't reason like a human. By the time NeMo gathered sufficient audio to classify reliably, the early labels were already fixed. The classifier didn't go back to edit.
Misclassification — Boeing MCAS (2018) ▼
Background: Boeing's 737 MAX included a new flight control system called Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS), designed to correct for the aircraft's rushed engineering that created a tendency for the plane to pitch upward under certain conditions.

MCAS took input from a single sensor. Should that sensor fail, MCAS took authority over the airplane and put it into a dive. Pilots fought to regain control, but MCAS repeatedly re-engaged and overpowered them. 346 people died across two crashes.
Audio Pipeline: NeMo's early-episode embeddings are that single sensor. One data source, immature, given full authority over speaker assignment. The classifier doesn't cross-check against a second input or flag low confidence. It commits and moves on.
Key Difference: Boeing's failure had a body count. A mislabeled training set has a quality count — quieter, slower, and easier to miss. The mechanism is the same: single input, high confidence, no verification loop, errors that compound downstream.

Why Did This Happen?

WhisperX performs transcription in batches. When the batch size is large, short utterances surrounded by laughter, cross-talk, or music can fall below decoding confidence and disappear. Reducing the batch size changes the acoustic context and allows the segment to surface.

Speaker classification introduces the second failure mode. Early in an episode, NeMo has minimal information about each speaker's voice. When Friedberg speaks at 21 seconds, the model has not yet heard a clear, audible example of Brad Gerstner. The system assigned Friedberg's voice to the closest available cluster — which at the time was Brad Gerstner. Cosine similarity showed the segment is closest to Friedberg's voice.

As the episode advances, speaker embeddings mature and cluster representation shifts — however, the earlier mislabeling is never revisited. The transcript appears complete. The pipeline exits cleanly. The misattribution remains hidden.

Implications for Training Data

Every misattributed line becomes training data. Speaker attribution errors rarely surface as failures — the transcript appears correct and the pipeline produces no warnings. Incorrect labels quietly propagate into downstream datasets.

Two-pass diarization addresses part of the problem, and human-in-the-loop labeling can catch residual errors. But the deeper issue is architectural: single-pass diarization on long-form audio is biased against early segments.

The first minute of a three-hour episode is labeled with dramatically less information than the final hour. Across hundreds of episodes, that asymmetry compounds.
