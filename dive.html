03 / Forensics

Ghosts in the Audio

I built an AI audio pipeline to generate transcripts from the All-In Podcast — transcripts that would go through a labeling process and become training data for host persona chatbots.

Significant forensic care went into HITL tooling and heuristics to guard against common AI mistakes, particularly speaker misclassification — speech wrongly attributed to the wrong person. The most consequential failure mode arrived late, after switching from low-quality YouTube rips to higher-quality episode files from Libsyn. I've coined it ghost audio: speech that goes untranscribed, creating gaps in conversational dialogue. The model learns to respond to fragmented, incomplete information. It doesn't know what it missed. Neither do you, unless you catch it.

Here's an example of both failure modes, using movies.

The Scene

Episode 136 opens without Jcal, the podcast's self-described best moderator in the world. Chamath fills in around t=12s, mimicking Jcal's speech mannerisms and roasting him. Everyone laughs.

Around t=21s, post-laughter, Friedberg mutters "Oh my God" under his breath. This utterance had been transcribed months earlier from a low-quality YouTube rip — but was absent from the fresh Libsyn run. That absence was jarring. The omission of "Oh my God" wouldn't derail Friedberg's training data on its own. But it suggested a novel failure mode that had gone undetected for months — and I was committed to ground truth.

Discovery

Paradoxically, the ghost segment appeared in the older transcript from the low-quality YouTube rip, but not the higher-quality Libsyn file. More content, larger file size — you'd expect better results. Instead, the YouTube rip's larger dynamic range made voice detection possible at the ghost segment where the Libsyn file couldn't.

Forensic Analysis

The VAD score measures how confident the model is that someone is speaking — 0.50 is the speech/silence threshold. Around t=20–21s, both files show a dip after Chamath finishes. In the YouTube file, that dip crosses below 0.50 — Silero cuts a segment boundary, and "Oh, my God" lands at the top of a fresh segment with clean context. In the Libsyn file, the score never crosses the threshold. No boundary is cut. The ghost arrives inside a long, dense chunk and gets deprioritized.

A Happy Penicillin Moment

While working through the f0 findings, Claude regenerated the WhisperX script — but with large-v2 rather than the large-v3 model I'd been running. Whether I let it run before catching the change or noticed it afterward, the result was the same: large-v2 surfaced Friedberg's ghost. Timestamps required manual correction via Audacity — but the speech was there.

The ghost was recoverable through a model swap that happened by accident, after deliberate forensic methods came up short, it shows dumb luck has a seat at the table.

Why Did V2 Work? The Beam Search Autopsy

The first question: did the model even attend to the ghost segment? The attention distribution graph below plots where Whisper focused during transcription. The orange spikes are attention — each peak is the model actively weighting a moment in the audio. The three green dashed lines mark the ground truth positions of "Oh," "my," and "god" at t=21–22s. The ghost has attention. It's just small, dwarfed by the surrounding spikes. The model looked at Friedberg's utterance and kept moving.

Image attention.png

This rules out the simplest explanation. The failure wasn't deafness. The audio was physically present (VAD confirmed it), the model processed it (attention confirmed it), and it still didn't make it into the transcript. Something downstream of perception made the decision to drop it. That something was beam search.

Beam search holds multiple candidate sequences in parallel and scores them cumulatively. The highest-scoring sequence wins. Friedberg's "Oh my God" was quiet and hesitant. Its opening token — "Oh" — scored low enough that beam search pruned the entire path in favor of Friedberg's subsequent, more dominant speech around t=23s, which carried a stronger cumulative score.

The failure was compound. V3 dropped the ghost only when all four conditions were present simultaneously — remove any one and the ghost reappears:

Compound failure — remove any one condition and the ghost reappears
1 Whisper large-v3 not v2
2 WhisperX pipeline not raw transformers
3 without_timestamps=True WhisperX default
4 beam_size ≥ 5 WhisperX default

With without_timestamps=False, timestamp tokens get inserted between words during generation. These tokens carry high probability by definition — the model is confident about when something was said even when uncertain about what. That confidence acts as a scoring cushion, keeping lower-probability word paths alive in the beam long enough to compete. V2's probability distribution is flatter — "Oh my God" stayed competitive without the boost. V3's is sharper, more peaked around dominant audio. Without the cushion, the ghost path got pruned. WhisperX running v3 on default settings was systematically dropping quiet, hesitant speech. Not randomly. Predictably.

Research note At one point during testing, setting no_speech_threshold = 0.3, log_prob_threshold = -1.0, and batch_size = 8 surfaced Friedberg's segment in the Libsyn transcript. The result did not reproduce on subsequent runs. The parameters were logged — the discrepancy remains unexplained.

The Lesson

AI can be confidently incorrect, which breeds complacency in its operators. These failure modes were found through forensic review — listening to raw audio, watching video, comparing pipeline runs. The ghost segment's omission passed silently. Discovering it required spotting a discrepancy between runs — a needle-in-a-haystack event given there are half a million such segments across 358 episodes.

HITL validation isn't optional. Automated pipelines succeed while dropping real speech. The only defense is systematic manual review — and even that won't catch everything.

Useful analogies — ghost audio & misclassification
Ghost Audio — Crimson Tide (film)▼
Crimson Tide
In the 1995 film Crimson Tide, the submarine USS Alabama receives an Emergency Action Message ordering a nuclear launch. A second EAM follows but cuts off mid-transmission. The Captain assumes the prior order stands; the First Officer believes the second may contain a cancellation. The crew splits.
Audio Pipeline Friedberg's "Oh, my God" appeared in an earlier transcript run but was absent in subsequent runs against the Libsyn file. The signal exists — spectral subtraction confirms it — but a reliable pipeline path to recover it has not been found. The radio didn't fail. The message was received. In the noise, no one noticed it arrive.
Key Difference — Visibility In Crimson Tide, everyone knows the record is incomplete. In the audio pipeline, the omission passes silently. Discovering it required spotting a discrepancy between runs.
Misclassification — Boeing MCAS (2018)▼
Background
Boeing's MCAS relied on a single angle-of-attack sensor. When that sensor failed, MCAS repeatedly pushed the aircraft nose-down. Two crashes. 346 people died.
Audio Pipeline NeMo's speaker embeddings are the single sensor: a low-signal embedding carries the same authority as a high-signal one. Early in an episode, NeMo has heard very little of each speaker. When Friedberg speaks at 21 seconds, his voice is assigned to the closest available cluster — Gerstner. The misattribution is never revisited. Errors propagate downstream.
Key Difference — Consequence Boeing's failure had a body count. A mislabeled training set has a quality count — quieter, slower, easier to miss. Same mechanism: single input, high confidence, no verification loop, errors that compound.
