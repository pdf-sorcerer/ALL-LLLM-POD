Project Overview

Four Hosts, One Pipeline

I built chatbots of the four All-In Podcast hosts — Jason Calacanis, David Sacks, David Friedberg, and Chamath Palihapitiya — by transcribing 538 podcast episodes (460 hours of audio) and using those transcripts to give each persona its voice.

The chatbots run on my iPhone with text and voice; a local PC handles the heavy GPU work.

The chatbots are fun. The transcripts turned out to be more interesting.

I've put together two versions of this writeup — a casual reader that walks through what I dug into and why it mattered, and a technical cut with the system internals. Switch between them below.

01

The Thing I Wasn't Looking For

While building v2 of the transcripts — moving from YouTube audio to higher-quality Libsyn files — I was comparing Episode 136 side-by-side. In the opening 30 seconds, Friedberg says "oh my god." It's in the YouTube transcript. It's not in the Libsyn one.

Three missing words. Not a catastrophe. But the failure mode bothered me: same speech, transcribed in one file, dropped in the other. Same model. Same code. Different audio. I started calling these ghosts — audible speech that exists in the recording but doesn't reach the transcript.

Misclassification (right words, wrong speaker) is bad. Ghosts are worse. Ghosts erase content entirely. For chatbots trained on hundreds of transcripts, a missing reaction changes what each persona appears to think and how they respond. I paused v2 and asked a different question: how do I detect ghosts at scale across 538 episodes?

02

The Two Ghosts in Episode 136

Episode 136 opens with Friedberg moderating for an absent Jason Calacanis. Chamath roasts Jcal — mimicking him, escalating it. The room laughs. Then two reactions land back-to-back:

Chamath: "...and the fattest, that's the dumbest." [laughter, music] Friedberg: [MISSING: "Oh my god"] Friedberg: "He's not even here. You can't do that. It's awful." Gerstner: [MISSING: "Oh my god"] Friedberg: "That's just bullying. It's awful."

Two ghosts, side by side. They look the same in writing. They're completely different problems.

03

What the Audio Said vs. What the Model Heard

I checked whether the audio actually contained speech in those windows. It did. Spectrograms showed clear vocal energy. Pitch detection found a fundamental frequency. After stripping out the dominant audio (Chamath's voice, the music) with spectral subtraction, Friedberg's vocal pitch surfaced cleanly inside his ghost window.

So the audio existed. The transcription failed.

I dug into Whisper's decoder — the part of the model that picks which words to emit. At each step it considers thousands of candidate next-words and picks the most likely one. I extracted what it was thinking at each ghost moment.

  • Friedberg: "Oh my god" was the runner-up. The decoder considered it at about 4% probability. The dominant choice — "He's not even" — was 16× more likely, so that's what got emitted. Friedberg's actual next sentence won the beam. His reaction got skipped over.
  • Gerstner: "Oh my god" wasn't even in contention. It ranked between 15th and 134th out of the 50,000 possible next-words, with probabilities around 0.01%. The dominant path was 10,000× more likely. The decoder didn't almost-emit Gerstner's words. It never considered them.
Two failure modes, one costume
Friedberg-class ghosts are recoverable — the decoder almost got there. Gerstner-class ghosts are unrecoverable — the decoder doesn't know there's anything there at all.
04

The Pipeline That Recovers Friedberg

After a long detour testing decoder configurations (beam size, timestamp settings, batch sizes — all of which mattered in narrow ways but none of which solved the underlying problem), the answer turned out to be structural, not parametric.

Whisper makes a quiet decision at the start of decoding: how densely to segment the audio. On default 30-second chunks, it picks a sparse mode that absorbs quiet utterances into longer segments — Friedberg's "oh my god" gets swallowed by his next sentence. On smaller chunks set by a voice-activity detector, it picks dense mode and Friedberg gets his own slot.

So I switched pipelines. Out: WhisperX (the off-the-shelf tool I'd been using). In: a hybrid of pyannote (voice activity detection) → reference openai-whisper (the original Python implementation, not the speed-optimized fork) → wav2vec2 (word-level alignment).

I call this hybrid pww. It costs about 10 minutes per episode instead of 1–2 — roughly 60 GPU-hours to reprocess the corpus instead of 10. Worth it. Friedberg recovers reliably. Gerstner still doesn't, because Gerstner's problem isn't segmentation density — it's that he's below the decoder's noise floor entirely.

05

A Second Failure Mode: Speaker Attribution

Even when the words come back correctly, the speaker can be wrong. Episode 136's Friedberg ghost — once recovered by pww — still got attributed to Chamath by the speaker-identification model (NeMo). At 0.6 seconds, the utterance is too short for the model to build a distinctive voiceprint. It gets compared to whichever speaker centroid is closest, and small differences flip the assignment.

I tested an obvious fix: wait until you've seen the whole episode, build mature centroids from all the data, then go back and re-classify everything against those. Two variants tested. Neither helped. On a 9-segment ground-truth slice, the rescoring pass was 5/8 correct vs. the first pass's 7/8. It introduced regressions and didn't fix the original miss.

The reason it failed is structural: when the first pass is mostly correct, averaging across its labels produces centroids that sit closer together than truth — especially when speaker manifolds overlap. Small embedding differences flip assignments at the fuzzy boundary. The fix made things worse.

So: trust the first pass. Flag short segments (under 4 seconds) for human review regardless of the model's confidence. The 0.6-second ghost is below the threshold where the math can help; that's a human-in-the-loop problem, not a clustering problem.

06

Three Detectors, Three Failure Modes

The investigation produced a taxonomy of how transcripts go wrong, plus a detection mechanism for each:

  • Hallucination loops. When the decoder skips real audio, it often fills the silence with a repeated phrase from its language model. Episode 136 has "I'm bullying when you're bullying" emitted three times in 10 seconds — none of it in the audio. Detected by scanning for exact-match phrases that repeat within short windows, then confirming with a forced-alignment check that the phonemes don't match the underlying audio.
  • v1↔v2 transcript diffs. Where two pipelines transcribe the same episode and disagree, the disagreements are diagnostic. New content in v2 = recovered ghost. Content in v1 that's gone in v2 = suppressed hallucination. I built a tool called littlecat that diffs two transcripts segment-by-segment and surfaces the meaningful differences — filtering out segmentation noise, speaker-name aliases, contractions, number-vs-word spellings. On Episode 136 it cut 1,316 raw differences down to 22 reviewable entries — about a 98% noise reduction. The 22 contained the original Friedberg ghost plus several de novo ghosts neither pipeline alone had surfaced.
  • Embedding triple-negatives. This one's still a sketch. The speaker-identification model produces voice embeddings on a regular grid — every fraction of a second, regardless of what the transcription pipeline does. Where a high-magnitude, speech-like embedding exists at a timestamp that has no transcript segment AND no speaker turn, that triple-negative is a candidate Gerstner-class ghost. The embedding is evidence something happened acoustically; both downstream pipelines threw it away. This complements the hallucination detector: hallucinations show where the decoder spoke too much, embedding triple-negatives show where it spoke too little.
07

The Repair Tool: brody

Once detection surfaces a candidate, a human has to confirm and fix it. That's brody, a Streamlit app with two tabs:

  • Tab 1 — Speaker mapping. After the speaker-ID model runs, brody groups segments by cluster ID, plays the five longest samples per cluster, and lets you type a human name. Names propagate to every segment in the cluster.
  • Tab 2 — Lexical review. Twelve flags surface candidate problems: lowercase-continuation (segment opens lowercase after a non-terminal-punctuation segment — likely a continuation misattributed across a speaker boundary), backchannel-bleed (short "yeah"/"right"/"totally" attributed to the wrong speaker), blob (segments over 20 seconds, likely containing multiple speakers), low-score (wav2vec confidence under 0.5, correlates with quiet audio or overlap), and others. Each flag is a deterministic rule, fully readable, fully editable.

Filter, page through, listen, edit, save. The full pipeline produces flagged candidates; brody is where humans reduce them to corrected transcripts.

08

Where It Lands

The pipeline now looks like this:

pww transcription ↓ littlecat diff (catches recoverable ghosts) ↓ NeMo speaker ID ↓ brody review (catches misattributions) ↓ consecutive-same-speaker batching ↓ chatbot training data

For 538 episodes, the manual review surface comes out to roughly 22 candidates per episode × 538 ≈ 12,000 entries to human-review across the corpus. That's tractable. Without these filters it would be every segment of every episode — millions.

What this is and isn't
Some ghosts (Gerstner-class) remain unrecoverable. Some short utterances will keep getting misattributed because the math literally can't tell. The investigation produced a detection layer, not a perfect transcript — but a corpus that flags its own failures is a categorically different thing from one that pretends they aren't there.

The goal is for each chatbot persona to be trained on what they said in the episode, and that comes down to the accuracy of transcript speaker labeling. Two challenges emerged and need to be addressed:

  • Misattribution — speech correctly transcribed but assigned to the wrong speaker. Trains one host in knowledge they never had; denies the rightful host their own content.
  • Ghost audio (late discovery). Audible speech that does NOT appear in the transcript. Silently distorts conversational dialogue. Personas don't learn what they said, and what they say or respond to is warped by the ghost's omission of content.
Baseline
Both humans and AI struggle with short-form conversational audio, especially burst speech, soft utterances, and cross-talk. Training a custom speech classifier, I found a 12% classification error rate — better than what's reported elsewhere. However, actors in this space commonly drop segments shorter than 5 seconds; that would be 43% of all v1 content.
01

Discovery (Episode 136, Opening 30 Seconds)

Trigger. I was creating a version 2 (v2) of episode transcripts using Libsyn audio for easier long-term management. For Episode 136, I opened a v1 transcript with a fresh v2 transcript side-by-side and noticed a line missing from David Friedberg ("oh my god").

Observation. Friedberg's "Oh my god" at ~21s was present in the YouTube transcript, absent in the Libsyn transcript. On closer inspection, a second "Oh my god" from Brad Gerstner at ~28s — absent from both transcripts and only audible by watching the video and noticing his lips move.

Implication. The value of the completions ("oh my god") to host corpora is probably very low. But the failure mode is potentially serious: an omission of content isn't just denying the host their spoken words — it potentially warps how the model learns knowledge.

Audio verification:

  • Spectrogram showed clear speech energy in both ghost regions
  • RMS in Friedberg's window: 81.8th percentile of surrounding 10s
  • F0 detection confirmed vocal fundamental in both windows
  • Conclusion: voice exists; transcription failure not yet localized

This converted the question from "is the audio there" to "what in the pipeline is dropping it."

The Libsyn file's tighter dynamic range smoothed the VAD curve, the dip disappeared, and the segment was absorbed into the surrounding region.

2.2 Voice activity detection

Silero VAD ran across both files. Friedberg's window showed continuous speech in both. Gerstner's window showed discontinuous speech in both, with a characteristic dropout between bursts.

Result: positive but not conclusive. VAD detected speech in both ghost windows. The downstream transcriber was told "voice exists in these places," but it transcribed nothing. What was doing that?

2.3 Spectral subtraction

To test whether ghost speech was acoustically masked by the dominant audio:

  • Built a noise/voice profile from a clean sample of Chamath's roast (17–19s)
  • Subtracted Chamath's profile from the full 30-second segment window
  • Used pYIN pitch detection on the residual audio

Results:

  • pYIN frame count: 81 (raw) → 146 (residual)
  • New F0 cluster emerged at ~21s — Friedberg's vocal fundamental, inside his ghost window
  • Gerstner's window: no pitch revealed

Friedberg's voice was acoustically present but masked. Gerstner's was either masked beyond what spectral subtraction could recover or genuinely below the level the technique can isolate.

2.4 Whisper cross-attention

Extracted decoder cross-attention weights for the 30-second window and plotted against time:

  • Friedberg's ghost (~20–22.5s): wide attention valley, near-zero values
  • Gerstner's ghost (~28s): narrow gap sandwiched between two of the strongest attention spikes in the window

Near-zero attention is consistent with the decoder ignoring the region. This explained the symptom but not the cause — why does the decoder distribute attention this way on this audio when the YouTube version got it right?

03

Decoder Autopsy (Beam Search)

At each generation step, Whisper's decoder picks one of ~50,000 possible tokens. Beam search keeps the top-N candidate sequences alive simultaneously. I ran beam=5 and inspected the candidates at each ghost decision point.

Friedberg's ghost (prefix: "...that's the dumbest."):

  • Dominant beam: "He's not even" — 67.82% joint probability
  • "Oh my god" beam: 4.22% joint probability
  • Ratio: ~16× in favor of the dominant path

"He's not even here" is what Friedberg actually said immediately after the ghost. The decoder saw the ghost as a plausible runner-up but committed to the higher-probability path.

Gerstner's ghost (prefix: "...It's awful."):

  • Dominant beam: "That's just bullying" — 89.80% joint probability
  • "Oh my god" beam: 0.00896% joint probability
  • Ratio: ~10,000× in favor of the dominant path

"Oh my god" wasn't a runner-up; it wasn't in contention.

Two-class taxonomy crystallized
Friedberg-class: subthreshold but adjacent. The decoder almost got there. Recovery is achievable if the pipeline can nudge segmentation toward giving the ghost its own decode slot.

Gerstner-class: subthreshold by orders of magnitude. The decoder doesn't see the audio at all in any meaningful sense. Recovery requires something other than decoder tuning — likely pipeline architecture changes upstream of the decoder, or HITL review against external evidence (embeddings).
04

Configuration Sweeps and the WhisperX Dead-End

4.1 Phase 1–5: variable isolation in WhisperX

Tested decoder/pipeline variables across 100 trials (5 configs × 20 trials) in WhisperX:

Config Setup Friedberg Recovery
A WhisperX + large-v3 + beam=5 + no-timestamps 0/20
B WhisperX + large-v2 + same flags 20/20
C Raw transformers (no WhisperX) 20/20
D WhisperX + v3 + beam=5 + with-timestamps 20/20
E WhisperX + v3 + beam=1 + no-timestamps 20/20

The compound failure was WhisperX + large-v3 + without_timestamps=True + beam≥5. Break any one of those four conditions and the ghost surfaces. 100% deterministic on the test sample.

4.2 Gerstner: nothing works

Same matrix expanded to 160 trials covering raw Whisper without VAD, multiple beam sizes, multiple timestamp modes, both v2 and v3:

  • Friedberg: 0/160 in raw Whisper without VAD (he needs WhisperX's VAD-driven chunking to surface)
  • Gerstner: 0/160 across every configuration tested. No combination of settings recovers him.

4.3 The pivot

Phase 1–5 produced reproducible findings inside WhisperX, but the framing was wrong. Beam size and timestamp settings matter only because they sit downstream of a more fundamental decision: how the decoder segments at step 0.

Lockstep greedy decoding of the same audio under two chunk configurations revealed:

  • Default 0–30s chunk → 8 timestamps emitted across 93 decoder steps. Friedberg's window absorbed into a long "He's not even here" segment. Sparse-segmentation mode.
  • Pyannote chunks (1.26–30.98s) → 45 timestamps emitted. Friedberg gets his own segment slot. Dense-segmentation mode.

Cosine similarity of encoder features between the two configurations: 0.90. The encoder sees nearly the same audio. The decoder makes a different segmentation choice at step 0, and that choice cascades.

Implication: chasing CTranslate2's beam-size sensitivity was the wrong target. The right target is upstream — make sure the decoder gets the audio in chunks that trigger dense mode.

05

The pww Pipeline

New pipeline:

pyannote VAD (small chunks, ~1–30s, aligned to speech) ↓ reference openai-whisper (PyTorch, beam=1, greedy, temperature=0) ↓ wav2vec2 forced alignment (word-level timestamps + per-word confidence)

Validation on full 98-minute Episode 136:

VAD Scale Friedberg Gerstner Total Time
pyannote30s chunk✓✗37.6s
pyannotefull✓✗575.5s
Silero30s chunk✓✗93.2s
Silerofull✓✗625.9s
  • Friedberg recovers in all four configurations. Given dense-mode chunks, the decoder reaches his decode state.
  • Gerstner drops in all four. Confirms the two-class distinction is real, not artifactual.
  • VAD choice doesn't matter for ghost recovery on this episode — pyannote and Silero both produce chunks that trigger dense mode.
  • Pyannote VAD is 13× faster than Silero (4.7s vs 69.6s on a 98-minute file). Pyannote wins on cost.
  • ASR dominates wall time (~90% of total).
  • Cost vs. WhisperX default: ~10 min/episode for pww vs. ~1–2 min for WhisperX CTranslate2. Across 538 episodes: ~90 GPU-hours instead of ~15. Tradeoff is compute time for content fidelity.

5.1 Determinism check

Same audio chunk fed to reference Whisper twice in a single process:

  • Encoder features bit-exact identical (max abs diff = 0.0e+00)
  • Token sequences identical at every step (196/196 tokens match)
  • Final text byte-identical

Reference openai-whisper is fully deterministic at temperature=0, beam=1 on this hardware. Earlier observations of "different hallucinations between runs" came from process-state pollution when multiple pipelines loaded sequentially, not from Whisper nondeterminism.

06

Hallucination as Signal

In Gerstner's window, all three pipelines (WhisperX, pww-pyannote, pww-Silero) emit the same hallucinated phrase: "I'm bullying when you're bullying" or close variants, repeated 3 times in ~10 seconds. The phrase isn't in the audio. Wav2vec2 forced alignment fails on it across all three pipelines — phonemes don't match.

I had a hunch this could be exploitable as a detection mechanism. When VAD is applied before transcription, silence is cut out of the full audio file. It's common for Whisper to produce hallucinations during silent audio, but here there is none. So when the decoder gets stuck in a language-model loop, real audio is almost certainly being masked by hallucination. The signature — repeated phrase + alignment failure + VAD-voiced region — marks regions where transcription failed silently.

6.1 Repetition probe (stage 1)

Scanned the full 98-minute transcript for exact-match phrases appearing 2+ times within a 30-second window (≥4 words OR ≥20 chars). Found 9 candidate clusters:

  • 1 high-confidence ghost loop ("I'm bullying when you're bullying" ×3, gaps 4.0s + 2.9s)
  • 8 lower-confidence 2× repeats spread across the episode

6.2 Acoustic verification (stage 2)

Ran VAD/RMS/F0 probes on each occurrence. All 9 classified as real_repetition because the probes detect speech presence, not transcription correctness.

Limitation found: acoustic probes alone cannot distinguish hallucination-over-real-speech (the Gerstner case) from genuine repetition. The bullying hallucination occurs while Friedberg is actually speaking, so VAD/RMS/F0 all show real signal — they just don't know whose signal it is.

The negative finding was useful: hallucinations don't only happen over silence. They happen over real speech that the decoder ignores in favor of its own language-model continuation. This forced the realization that hallucination detection needs phonemic alignment (wav2vec2 failure), not just acoustic presence.

07

Speaker Attribution Layer

Even when text is recovered, the speaker label can be wrong. NeMo's clustering makes incremental decisions as TitaNet embeddings arrive; speaker centroids are immature in early-episode windows. A short utterance that lands during the immaturity window risks being assigned to whichever centroid was closest at the moment of decision — which may not be the right one.

7.1 Pass 1 vs. Pass 2 hypothesis

Hypothesis: if Pass 1's centroids are immature, a Pass 2 that builds centroids from the full episode and re-classifies every segment should outperform Pass 1.

Test slice: 9 segments from Episode 136 (16.5–30.5s) with ground-truth labels — 3 Chamath, 6 Friedberg, including the "Oh my god" ghost.

Pass 1 results (NeMo raw clustering, pyannote-derived UEM):

  • 7/8 correct (1 unknown)
  • The single miss: Friedberg's "Oh my god" at 20.871s assigned to Chamath
  • This is the 0.6-second utterance — exactly the case Pass 2 was designed to fix

Pass 2 v5c (centroid rescoring, full-episode means):

  • 5/8 correct
  • The headline ghost case did not improve (still misclassified)
  • Two previously-correct classifications regressed:
    • "And the fattest, that's the dumbest" (Chamath ✓ → speaker_3 ✗)
    • "You can't do that" (Friedberg ✓ → speaker_2 ✗)
  • Net: 0 fixes, 2 regressions

7.2 Why Pass 2 failed

Structural reason: when Pass 1's labels are mostly correct, averaging across them produces centroids that sit closer together than they should — especially when speaker manifolds overlap (Chamath and Friedberg do, visibly, in the embedding viz). Full-episode averaging pulls both centroids toward the overlap zone. Segments that previously sat cleanly inside one cluster now sit near a fuzzy boundary, and small embedding differences flip assignments.

Centroid rescoring is most useful when initial labels are noisy enough that averaging pulls them toward truth. Here, initial labels were clean enough that averaging just smeared the boundaries.

7.3 Decision

Drop Pass 2. Trust Pass 1. Centroids still computed (cheap, useful for the manifold viz's conf value) but inform diagnostic flags only — not classification decisions.

The 0.6-second ghost case is below the threshold where TitaNet produces a speaker-distinctive embedding regardless of which centroid you compare it against. That's a different problem from "the centroid is immature." The right tool for this segment class is HITL review surfaced via a lizard flag (segments under 4s flagged regardless of model confidence).

Failure Taxonomy and Detection Mechanisms

The investigation produced two orthogonal failure modes plus a hybrid case, and one detection mechanism for each.

8.1 Lexical / transcription failures

Failure Fingerprint Detector
Friedberg-class ghost v1/v2 disagreement; segment in one pipeline, absent in other littlecat (segment-level diff)
Hallucination loop Exact-match phrase repeated within short window + wav2vec alignment failure on phonemes Repetition scanner + alignment-failure flag
Gerstner-class ghost TitaNet embedding magnitude high at a timestamp with no transcript segment AND no RTTM turn Embedding triple-negative (sketched, not yet built)

8.2 Diarization / attribution failures

Six diarization failure patterns currently surface in HITL review. The published page categorizes them as fingerprints; brody operationalizes them as flags.

Pattern Fingerprint Brody Flag(s)
1a. Turn contamination: ellipsis One segment contains two speakers; mid-segment ellipsis is the seam ellipsis
1b. Speaker sandwich A→B→A where middle B is misattribution; mid-segment ellipsis + next opens lowercase ellipsis + lowercase-continuation
2. Third-person self-reference Speaker's own name appears in their own segment Not yet implemented
3. Lowercase continuation New segment opens lowercase — ASR capitalizes new turns, so lowercase = continuation misattributed lowercase-continuation, lowercase-start
4. Backchannel bleed Short "yeah"/"right"/"totally" assigned to acoustically dominant local speaker backchannel-bleed + lizard (<4s)
5. Blob (speaker collapse) 20+ second segment attributed to one speaker but containing multiple blob (>20s)

Brody also implements flags that don't map 1:1 to a published failure mode but surface candidates for review:

  • hyphen-split — hyphen+space inside a segment (often a turn boundary the splitter didn't catch)
  • no-terminal-punct — segment lacks .?! ending; adjacent to ellipsis as boundary signal
  • noise (<0.02s) — sub-perceptual segments, almost certainly artifacts
  • discourse-marker — segment opens with so/well/anyway/etc. (pair-aware; skipped at episode start)
  • period-before-conjunction — prev ends terminal, current opens with and/but/etc., suggesting a sentence cut across a speaker boundary
  • low-score (<0.5) — wav2vec mean per-word score below 0.5; correlates with quiet audio, overlap, or noise

The low-score flag is notable: it was first identified as an open thread in littlecat's design (distinguish recovered ghost from hallucinated micro-segment) and ended up implemented one stage downstream, in brody, after NeMo runs. The "LLM." / "a tube." class of micro-segments gets caught here.

09

The Tools

9.1 littlecat — v1↔v2 transcript diff

Compares two transcripts of the same audio segment-by-segment and surfaces meaningful differences. Pipeline order:

  • Time-overlap matching — pairs v1↔v2 segments by best time-window overlap (default tolerance 0.5s). One-to-one matching; leftovers become candidate v1_only / v2_only.
  • Three-tier text classification for matched pairs: identical_text (lowercase + whitespace match) → near_identical (also strip basic punctuation) → catches normalization-equivalent variants (J.Cal/Jcal, co-founder/cofounder, A.I./AI).
  • Aggressive normalization for containment checks: lowercase → expand contractions (~50 entries including spoken fusions: gonna, gotta, wanna, kinda, lemme) → speaker aliases (Sax/Sachs/Saxx → sacks; analogous for Chamath, Friedberg) → compound aliases (~30 entries: news feed → newsfeed) → split mashed tokens → drop $ signs → digits to words → strip punctuation → post-strip aliases → collapse adjacent canonical-name duplicates.
  • Containment via Markov walk + LCS fallback — strip leading/trailing discourse markers from needle; pure-discourse needles preserved iff their tokens don't appear in nearby haystack (protects real "Yeah" / "Oh my god" ghost recoveries); Markov walk seeded at nearest-by-time segment with up to 1 zero-advance segment tolerance, max 5 hops; LCS time-window fallback if walk fails.
  • Block list drops segments containing known noise phrases (intro/outro music lyrics, recurring outro chatter), with optional last-N-minutes time gating.

~98% noise reduction. Of the final 22:

  • ~10 confirmed real signal, including four de novo ghosts (content authority v1 never surfaced at all): "Oh, my god." × 2 — Friedberg at 0:00:20.9 and Chamath at 1:27:45.8 — plus "Yeah, great." (0:02:10.5) and "LLM." (0:08:13.8). Plus genuine v1-only utterances v2 missed (Sacks "Totally.", "I got to go to Vegas.", "People were trying to shoot it down.") and stutter-fragment ghosts ("Can I ask you guys-", "So it sounds like the-").
  • ~3 accepted residuals: number paraphrases ($2.5 billion vs "two and a half billion"), homophones (errored/aired, base/basis).
  • ~5 missed false positives: cases requiring semantic/paraphrase understanding (single-content-word needles, word-order permutations, semantic paraphrases like "I was shocked" vs "Can't be anything but shocked").

Architecture is debuggable end-to-end. Every suppression decision can be traced through readable dictionaries and explicit thresholds. No opaque ML components. New variants (host nicknames, compound words, contractions) added by appending to the relevant dict and re-running.

Implication of the de novo ghosts
Authority v1 isn't editing these out — its upstream pipeline (VAD → diarization-first ASR) likely never surfaced them at all. Short emotional reactions ("Oh, my god."), low-amplitude micro-utterances ("LLM."), backchannels ("Yeah, great.") — exactly the classes hardest to cleanly diarize and most likely to overlap a dominant speaker. v2's pre-fusion ordering, transcribing first and aligning later, catches what v1's ordering excludes. These aren't HITL edit decisions; they're architectural blind spots. The Chamath "Oh, my god." at 1:27:45 lands in conversational context where the reaction is the punchline — without v2's recovery, the joke reads flat. v3 (the merged output) is the only path to recovering this content; the recurring pattern of the same phrases catching the same kinds of moments suggests a quantifiable corpus-wide pipeline-coverage gap.

Pipeline (Final)

audio ↓ pyannote VAD ↓ reference openai-whisper (beam=1, greedy, temperature=0) ↓ wav2vec2 forced alignment ↓ v2 transcript (segments with words[] and per-word scores) ↓ littlecat: v1↔v2 segment diff → HITL review of recovered ghosts / suppressed hallucinations ↓ NeMo Pass 1 clustering (pyannote-derived UEM) ↓ fused transcript (segments + nemo_speaker_id) ↓ brody Tab 1: speaker_id → human name mapping ↓ brody Tab 2: 12-flag lexical review → HITL corrections ↓ consecutive-same-speaker batching (utterance-level grouping) ↓ chatbot training data
  • Cost budget: ~10 minutes per episode wall time; ~90 GPU-hours total for 538 episodes.
  • HITL surface: ~22 littlecat candidates + brody flag counts per episode. At corpus scale, ~12k entries to review across 538 episodes — tractable.
11

Honest Caveats

  • Sample size. The 9-segment ground-truth slice is small. Pass 2 may help on segments outside this slice (later in episodes, longer utterances) that weren't checked. Net global outcome unverified.
  • Single-episode validation. Most pipeline validation lives on Episode 136. Generalization to noisier, longer, or multi-language episodes unverified. The Libsyn pipeline was a relatively clean test case (4 known speakers, ~98 min, podcast-quality).
  • Gerstner-class is unsolved. No tested pipeline configuration recovers Gerstner. The embedding triple-negative idea is sketched, not built, and may not work — it presumes TitaNet produces a high-magnitude embedding for sub-decoder-threshold audio, which is plausible but not yet verified.
  • Phase 1–5 trial findings remain historically true but no longer guide pipeline decisions. The pivot to reference Whisper on pyannote chunks bypasses CTranslate2 entirely, which is where the beam-size sensitivity lives.
  • Acoustic probes can't distinguish hallucination-over-speech from real repetition. The stage-2 probe verified this on Episode 136. Full hallucination detection requires wav2vec phonemic alignment failure, not VAD/RMS/F0 alone.
  • Heuristic thresholds are tuned by inspection. The 0.85 LCS coverage threshold, the 4-second lizard cutoff, the 20-second blob cutoff, the 0.5 wav2vec score floor — all hand-tuned on Episode 136 and similar test episodes. Edge cases will leak.
