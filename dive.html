Project Overview

Four Hosts, One Pipeline

I built chatbots of the four All-In Podcast hosts: Jason Calacanis, David Sacks, David Friedberg, and Chamath Palihapitiya, by first creating 538 podcast transcripts from episodes, clips, shorts, etc; (460 hours of host audio) and using those transcripts to give each persona its voice.

I can talk and text with the chatbots via my iPhone, and my remote PC handles all the heavy GPU work.

The chatbots are fun. The transcripts turned out to be more interesting.

I've put together two versions of this writeup — a casual reader that walks through what I dug into and why it mattered, and a technical cut with the system internals. Switch between them below.

01

The Discovery

In early 2026, I began work on v2 of the transcripts. My v1 corpus was a hodgepodge of videos, clips, and shorts from official All-In YouTube channels — brain-calorie intensive to maintain. Switching to Libsyn (the podcast provider with consistent naming) made things much easier.

Before committing fully to v2, I compared Episode 136 transcripts side by side. In the opening 30 seconds, both David Friedberg and Brad Gerstner say "oh my god". Friedberg's was in the original YouTube transcript but not the Libsyn copy. Gerstner's was in neither.

Same audio. Different transcript. That bothered me. My existing tools handled detectable AI quirks like speaker misclassification — wrong label, right words. But this was different. This was words that should be there, missing entirely. Ghost audio: audible speech that doesn't get transcribed, leaving silent gaps in the dialogue and distorting what the surrounding sentences appear to mean.

02

The Fix

The two ghosts look the same in writing. They turned out to be completely different problems.

Up to this point, all transcripts had been generated using a WhisperX pipeline, a derivative of OpenAI's Whisper automated speech recognition (ASR) model. WhisperX integrates voice-activity detection which curbs hallucinations, some neat word-level alignment tools, and also swaps Whisper's native decoder for a significantly faster CTranslate2 (anecdotally, about 5× faster generating transcripts). Think of it like an aftermarket exhaust for your car or motorcycle.

Both Whisper and WhisperX use the same model weights, and while their decoders differ, both build transcripts the same way: at each step, the decoder uses probability to pick the most likely next token from a vocabulary of ~50,000, emitting one at a time. Only Whisper's decoder will tell you what it was considering at the moment of choice. And I needed to know.

For Friedberg, " Oh" was the top candidate at 87.8% probability — the model almost emits the ghost on its own. It just doesn't get there in production. The original v2 transcript was generated by WhisperX — a popular community pipeline whose default backend is a high-performance C++ inference engine called CTranslate2. Same Whisper model weights, same pyannote chunk, but CT2's internal decoding decisions land somewhere different — and Friedberg gets dropped on the floor. Reference openai-whisper, given identical input, reaches the state where " Oh" sits at 87.8% and emits the ghost.

For Gerstner, " Oh" sat at rank 65 to 134, around 0.01–0.07%. Three orders of magnitude below Friedberg. The decoder doesn't almost-emit Gerstner — it never considers him.

Two failure modes, one costume
Friedberg-class ghosts are recoverable — the decoder almost gets there, and the right chunk boundaries push it the rest of the way. Gerstner-class ghosts are unrecoverable with current tools — the decoder doesn't know there's anything there at all.

After a long detour testing model variants, decoder configurations, and chunk strategies, the fix turned out to be structural: keep pyannote, keep wav2vec, but swap CTranslate2 for reference openai-whisper. The pipeline shape is the same as WhisperX — pyannote → reference openai-whisper → wav2vec2. I call it PWW.

It costs about 10 minutes per episode instead of 1–2 — roughly 90 GPU-hours to reprocess the corpus instead of 15. Worth it. Friedberg recovers reliably. Gerstner still doesn't, because his problem isn't the engine — he's below the decoder's noise floor entirely, no matter which Whisper backend runs the model. That one needs a different approach (sketched in the technical view).

03

Where It Lands

PWW recovers the words. But that's only half the job — the words still need the right speaker label, and Whisper still hallucinates in quiet stretches. The full pipeline pairs PWW with a transcript diff tool, a speaker-ID model, and a human review app:

pww transcription ↓ littlecat diff (catches recoverable ghosts) ↓ NeMo speaker ID ↓ brody review (catches misattributions) ↓ chatbot training data

For 538 episodes, manual review comes out to roughly 22 candidates per episode × 538 ≈ 12,000 entries across the corpus. Tractable. Without these filters it would be every segment of every episode — millions.

What this is and isn't
Some ghosts (Gerstner-class) remain unrecoverable. Some short utterances will keep getting misattributed because the math literally can't tell. The investigation produced a detection layer, not a perfect transcript — but a corpus that flags its own failures is a categorically different thing from one that pretends they aren't there.

Discovery

Building v2 transcripts of Episode 136 from a higher-quality Libsyn master, I noticed Friedberg's "Oh my god" at ~21s — present in the original YouTube transcript — was missing from the new one. On closer inspection a second "Oh my god" from Brad Gerstner at ~28s was absent from both transcripts. Acoustic verification confirmed speech energy in both windows: VAD positive, F0 fundamental recoverable for Friedberg via spectral subtraction, not for Gerstner. The audio is real. Something in the ASR pipeline is dropping it.

03

Decoder Probe — 4-Cell Variation Sweep

At each generation step the decoder picks one of ~50,000 tokens. The probability of any specific token — like " Oh" at the Friedberg moment — depends on the decoder state: which prefix tokens are committed, whether timestamps are enabled, and whether the decoder has built its own context from the audio or is being probed cold.

I ran a 4-cell sweep crossing timestamps ON/OFF with condition-on-previous-tokens (COPT) TRUE/FALSE, on both pyannote and Silero VAD chunks. V1 = TS on, COPT true (production-equivalent). V2 = TS on, COPT false (anchor-only). V3 = TS off, COPT true (continuation mode). V4 = TS off, COPT false (no context).

Chunk Cell TS COPT Reached " Oh" rank " Oh" prob
pyannote V1 ON TRUE ✓ 0 87.83%
pyannote V2 ON FALSE ✓ 0 34.15%
pyannote V3 OFF TRUE ✓ 0 58.93%
pyannote V4 OFF FALSE ✓ 46 0.020%
silero V1 ON TRUE ✗ 14 0.226%
silero V2 ON FALSE ✓ 0 49.06%
silero V3 OFF TRUE ✓ 0 57.61%
silero V4 OFF FALSE ✓ 48 0.019%

Results: " Oh" is rank 1 in 6 of 8 cells — the model knows the ghost is there in nearly every probe state. The chunk-sensitivity asymmetry shows up only in V1 (production-equivalent), where pyannote reaches " Oh" at 87.83% and Silero collapses to 0.226% despite identical audio content.

The finding behind the finding
Silero V1 = 0.226%. Silero V2 = 49.06%. Same chunk. Same audio at the encoder. The difference is whether the decoder builds 19 seconds of prefix context before probing. With pyannote's chunk, building context preserves the path to " Oh" (V1 = 87.83%). With Silero's chunk, building context destroys it (V1 = 0.226%). The chunk doesn't poison Whisper's perception of the audio — it poisons the natural-decode path through the audio.

Gerstner (same V1 probe state): rank 65–134 at 0.004–0.075% across all three chunks. None of the pipelines reach Gerstner's expected timestamp anchor in natural decode — all bypass it via an "I'm bullying when you're--" hallucination loop terminating at <|endoftext|>. Three orders of magnitude below Friedberg, and the decoder doesn't reach the relevant state regardless of which chunk it's handed.

Two failure modes, one costume
Friedberg-class ghosts are recoverable — the decoder almost gets there. In some pipeline configurations, chunk boundaries push it the rest of the way; in others, the same chunk fails when combined with the wrong inference engine and beam-search strategy. Of ten audio modifications tested on Libsyn — gain in either direction, dynamic-range expansion, EQ filters, loudness normalization — only one (a specific compression curve with gain) recovered Friedberg under default WhisperX. The failure mode is narrow and brittle, not a clean function of any one audio property. Gerstner-class ghosts are unrecoverable with current tools — the decoder doesn't know there's anything there at all, regardless of pipeline.
04

The PWW Pipeline

Out: WhisperX + CTranslate2. In: pyannote VAD → reference openai-whisper (PyTorch, beam=1, greedy, temperature=0) → wav2vec2 forced alignment.

audio ↓ pyannote VAD ↓ reference openai-whisper (beam=1, greedy, temperature=0) ↓ wav2vec2 forced alignment ↓ v2 transcript (segments + word timings + per-word scores)

Validated end-to-end on Episode 136. Friedberg recovers in all four configurations tested (pyannote/Silero × 30s-chunk/full-file). Gerstner drops in all four — the two-class distinction is real, not artifactual. Pyannote VAD is 13× faster than Silero and dominates on cost. ASR is ~90% of wall time.

Cost: ~10 min/episode vs ~1–2 min for WhisperX defaults. Across 538 episodes: ~90 GPU-hours instead of ~15. Tradeoff is compute time for content fidelity — and reference Whisper picks up more hallucinations than CTranslate2 in the process. Those hallucinations are detectable via wav2vec phonemic alignment failure, so the cost is bounded: the noise it picks up is flaggable.

Determinism: reference openai-whisper is bit-exact deterministic at temperature=0/beam=1 on this hardware (verified — encoder features identical, 196/196 tokens match, final text byte- identical). The chunk-boundary sensitivity in V1 is therefore not nondeterminism. Identical inputs produce identical outputs; similar inputs do not.

05

Caveats

  • Single-episode validation. Most pipeline validation lives on Episode 136. Generalization to noisier, longer, or multi-language episodes unverified.
  • Gerstner-class is unsolved. No tested configuration recovers him. The embedding triple-negative idea is sketched, not built.
  • The chunk-sensitivity finding lives at the isolated-probe level. V1 silero shows " Oh" at rank 14 / 0.226% in the stripped-down probe, but full-pipeline silero recovers Friedberg in production via fallback machinery (temperature schedules, surrounding context). The 4-cell sweep exposes trajectory fragility; production tooling absorbs much of it. Real, but its production-scale impact is bounded.
  • Heuristic thresholds tuned by inspection. 4-second lizard cutoff, 20-second blob cutoff, 0.5 wav2vec score floor — hand-tuned on Episode 136. Edge cases will leak.
