Ghosts in the Audio

I built chatbots of the four All-In Podcast hosts — Jason Calacanis, David Sacks, David Friedberg, and Chamath Palihapitiya — by creating, labeling, and processing transcripts from ### podcast episodes using an AI pipeline and human-in-the-loop tools for quality review. The personas run on my iPhone with text and voice, with GPU accelerated inference delegated to my local PC. The chatbots are neat, but the facinating part was the transcripts.

While building v2 of the transcripts — which moved from YouTube audio to higher-quality Libsyn files — I was comparing Episode 136 side-by-side. In the opening 30 seconds, an "oh my god" from Friedberg was in the YouTube transcript but missing from the Libsyn one. I call this Friedberg's ghost. Three missing words isn't a catastrophe, but the failure mode was troubling: the same speech transcribed in one file, dropped in the other. I call the phenomenon ghost audio: audible speech that exists in the recording but doesn't reach the transcript.

I paused v2 to chase a new goal: can I detect ghost audio at scale across 350+ episodes? The stakes aren't cosmetic. Unlike misclassification — where the right words go to the wrong speaker — ghost audio omits content entirely, silently distorting the shape of dialogue around every gap it creates. For chatbots trained on hundreds of transcripts, missing content changes what each persona appears to think and how they respond. Can't have that.

For additional insight into misclassification and ghost audio, I staged an iconic scene from Star Wars: Empire Strikes Back to showcase both — .

Ghost Audio Emerges: All-In Podcast Episode 136

Recap:

  • Ghost audio is audible speech that doesn't get transcribed.
  • First seen in Episode 136, comparing an original transcript to a new one.
  • No clue how pervasive it is, so I need to get a handle on that.
  • Friedberg's ghost — "Oh my god" — found in the original transcript, but not the new one.
  • Gerstner's ghost — "Oh my god" — also found moments after Friedberg's.

Episode 136 begins with Friedberg moderating for an absent Jason Calacanis (Jcal). Unsure how to open the show, Chamath opens by roasting Jcal — mimicking his speech and mannerisms. Laughter ensues, and music starts moments later.

Friedberg utters an "oh my god" — this is Friedberg's ghost. Gerstner says precisely the same thing moments later: "oh my god." Gerstner is barely audible; only watching video and studying Friedberg did I see that he spoke.

Transcript — ground truth
Chamath: "...and the fattest, that's the dumbest"
[laughter] [music]

Friedberg: [MISSING: "Oh my god"]
Friedberg: "He's not even here. You can't do that. It's awful."

Gerstner: [MISSING: "Oh my god"]
Friedberg: "That's just bullying. It's awful."

Roadmap for Analysis

  1. Audio files — compare the YouTube file to the newer Libsyn file.

  4. Model and Library Substitution — does swapping models and libraries have any influence?
  5. Beam Search Autopsy — inside the decoder, which candidate sequences were considered?

2 — Voice Activity Detection (VAD)

Because Friedberg's ghost wasn't transcribed in the Libsyn file, and Gerstner's wasn't transcribed in either, the question is: can the AI hear their voices in the audio?

I begin by using voice activity detection (VAD), which assigns each audio frame a probability score between 0 (silence) and 1 (active speech). VAD is a scout, labeling speech regions so the downstream AI transcriber knows what regions of the audio contain speech.

The graph below charts VAD output for both the original YouTube audio and the newer Libsyn file, across the 10-second window containing both Friedberg's and Gerstner's ghosts. The ghost windows — shaded in red/yellow — are envelopes of when each person spoke, determined in Audacity.

Results — inconclusive

Voice activity found in both ghost windows, but it's not clear who is speaking.

  • VAD shows continuous speech during Friedberg's ghost window.
  • VAD shows discontinuous speech during Gerstner's ghost window.

3 — Energy and Pitch Subtraction

The rest of this piece works exclusively with the Libsyn file.

Friedberg's ghost is audible, though easier to catch with headphones in a quiet room. Gerstner's ghost is nearly imperceptible. I only found it by accident — watching the podcast video while studying Friedberg.

After a couple hours, I had a hunch: could the ghosts be masked by the surrounding audio — laughter, music, and louder speech (albeit from Friedberg, who talks more in this span)?

I used spectral subtraction to test this. I built a profile of Chamath's voice from a clean sample (17–19s), then subtracted that profile from the full audio. The result is the residual — what's left once Chamath's acoustic signature has been removed.

The payoff is in column 2, row 3: pYIN jumps from 81 frames in the raw audio to 146 in the residual, and a new F0 cluster at ~21s where Friedberg's vocal fundamental emerges inside his ghost window (red band).

Conclusion

pYIN shows pitch exists in Friedberg's ghost envelope — but only emerges after the dominant audio is subtracted.

Next steps: what's the transformer's attention architecture doing?

4 — Whisper Decoder Cross-Attention

Whisper is an automatic speech recognition (ASR) model built on the transformer architecture. Transformers work in Whisper by selectively focusing on different parts of their audio through a mechanism called attention.

Attention has a finite budget that gets distributed across a span of audio. Dominant features get more attention; subtle features get less. The implication is that subtle features that get less attention make it much harder to transcribe audio in that space.

I extracted Whisper's cross-attention weights for the first 30 seconds of E136 and plotted them against time. Both ghost windows coincide with near-zero attention.

Observations.

  • Friedberg's ghost falls into a wide attention valley from roughly 20s to 22.5s.
  • Gerstner's ghost sits in a narrower gap, sandwiched between two of the strongest attention spikes in the window.

What this explains, and what it doesn't. Friedberg's ghost is recovered by the YouTube file, but not from the Libsyn file. Gerstner's is recovered from neither.

  • Using spectral subtraction, flickers of pitch emerge for Friedberg, not Gerstner.
  • Whisper cross-attention shows both ghost regions getting near-zero attention by the decoder.
  • Near-zero attention is a plausible explanation for why the decoder didn't transcribe both ghost regions.

5 — Beam Search Autopsy

Whisper's decoder emits tokens one at a time, picking from a vocabulary of ~50,000 possibilities at each step. I wanted to see which candidates were in contention at both ghost moments.

At each step, the decoder sees a prefix — the tokens already emitted — and picks the next token. Running beam-search with beam-size 5, the decoder keeps five candidate paths alive at each step rather than greedily committing to the single highest-probability next token.

  • Prefix for Friedberg's ghost: …that's the dumbest.
  • Prefix for Gerstner's ghost: …It's awful.

Friedberg's ghost "Oh my god." has a joint probability of 4.22% as a 4-token beam. The dominant beam at that moment, "He's not even," sits at 67.82% — about 16× higher. The ghost was a plausible runner-up, but the decoder picked the dominant path: "He's not even here" is what Friedberg actually said immediately after the ghost.

Gerstner's ghost "Oh my god" has a joint probability of 0.00896% across 3 tokens. The dominant beam "That's just bullying" sits at 89.80% — roughly 10,000× higher. Gerstner's ghost isn't a runner-up; it's not in the conversation.

Empire Strikes Back — staged demo

Two clips from Empire Strikes Back staged to show the failure modes of the AI that drives subtitle generation — just imagine that decades-old text transcripts were ruled cost-ineffective and replaced by generative AI streaming subtitles in real time.

Misclassification
Ghost audio

Using those videos' subtitles, we can create training data for speakers following a "prompt / completion" pairing. Here's what each failure mode produces from the same scene at the end of Empire:

Prompt
VADER: If you only knew the power of the dark side. Obi-Wan never told you what happened to your father.
LUKE: He told me enough! He told me you killed him.
Completion
YODA: No. I am your father.
Prompt
VADER: If you only knew the power of the dark side. Obi-Wan never told you what happened to your father.
Completion
LUKE: He told me enough! He told me you killed him. No. No. It's not true. That's not possible!

Almost everyone spots the Empire errors because we all know the source material. Across ### episodes of All-In, the same failure modes are running — just invisibly. The chatbot personas that emerge from the training data don't sound wrong. They respond like the hosts, and potentially know things the hosts never said.
