Speaker Identification Architecture

Speaker Classification Policy Evolution

V1 — Host-Only Classifier

The first iteration of a multi-speaker classifier was created using four binary classifiers to extract about 9,000 audio files for the hosts from 98 podcast episodes. WhisperX handled ASR processing, and Pyannote embeddings drove speaker mapping. These steps produced the first 187 transcripts for the app.

Noteworthy: Guests were deliberately excluded. Guest audio was difficult to reliably gather at scale. Despite this, the system did not misclassify guests as hosts — a fifth "unknown" class was unexpectedly created during training, and low-confidence guest segments were correctly absorbed into it. Even so, the system was basic and fragile, and required a redesign.

V1 — Core Signals
V1 Summary: • 9,000 host-only clips (98 episodes) using WhisperX + Pyannote • (Inexplicable) Guest Handling: "Unknown" class emerged to absorb guest audio • Output: First 187 functional transcripts Dataset Balance: • Sacks: 1602 | JCal: 1601 | Friedberg: 1573 | Chamath: 1810 → Achieved near-perfect host class balance Model Performance: • Best Model: MLP (97.9% Val / 0.953 Test Accuracy) • Sacks F1: 0.97 | JCal F1: 0.96 | Friedberg F1: 0.95 • Chamath F1: 0.94 (High Recall 0.99 → boundary over-calling) Key Insight: Confirmed host-only classification feasibility, but identified critical confusion between Chamath ↔ JCal due to fast-speech similarity. Architecture lacked production robustness.

V2 — Guest-Aware Scaling

V2 was made possible via a custom HITL tool I created called Clownfish. Clownfish ingested the original V1 transcripts and let me manually review and re-map Pyannote speaker_ids to custom_speaker labels, with side-by-side audio examples and video screengrabs for cross-validation. Proved to be an excellent, scalable process to find and extract host and guest audio. Once a speaker was validated and assigned, the transcript was automatically backfilled with the corrected labels. Audio for each segment was then extracted into clean, per-speaker directories. This produced the first reliable, guest-inclusive dataset and established a disciplined labeling pipeline.

Clownfish was so wildly effective, I began collecting more episodes growing. Also replaced Pyannote with Nvidia NeMo, which provided higher quality embeddings and restored confidence in the classification pipeline. Clusters became tighter, cosine distances became reliable, and label consistency improved across the entire dataset.

V2 — Core Signals
V2 Summary: • Integrated HITL pipeline (Clownfish) with clusterID/audio/video cross-validation • Massive scale-up: 85,619 total audio samples (70/15/15 split) • Transcript backfill applied after each speaker confirmation Dataset Distribution (Pre-balancing): • Chamath: 22,930 (26.8%) | JCal: 21,915 (25.6%) • Sacks: 13,696 (16.0%) | Friedberg: 13,103 (15.3%) • Guest: 13,975 (16.3%) → Introduced robust "Guest" class for negative sampling Performance Metrics: • Test Accuracy: 0.9798 (97.98%) • Sacks F1: 0.9851 | Chamath F1: 0.9823 • Guest F1: 0.9879 (Highest recall at 0.9895) • JCal F1: 0.9708 (Main source of noise: 48 misclassified as Chamath) Key Shift: V2 moved from simple MLP feasibility to a higher-quality, pre-production pipeline. While training hit 100% accuracy, the test set's 98% performance across 12,843 samples proved the model's ability to generalize once guest segments and class-weight balancing were properly implemented, ensuring evaluation metrics were no longer inflated by host-only domain constraints.

V4 — Duration Constraint & Production Reality

Whitepapers warn that sub-5s audio destabilizes embeddings, and this matched what I observed in my corpus.

Community advice was to drop short segments entirely — which makes sense: shorter audio is more likely to be mislabeled, and contains less acoustic identity. But roughly 50% of All-In segments are under 4 seconds, and discarding half the dataset this early wasn't acceptable.

My working hypothesis became:
1. Train the classifier only on host and guest audio longer than 5 seconds
2. Limit the classifier to segments longer than 5s, leaving all short segments untouched
3. Propagate speaker labels to short segments using NeMo cluster_id, which had proven remarkably reliable across dozens of episodes

Classifier Removal — Structural Failure

V4 was retired when embedding geometry made the core problem undeniable. Brad Gerstner's voice didn't sit in guest space — it overlapped directly with host clusters. His minimum cosine distance to JCal (0.2507) fell below JCal's own intra-class baseline (0.4842). His distances to Sacks (0.7774) and Friedberg (0.8007) both landed below the 0.8740 inter-host floor. No classifier trained on this label space could resolve those cases — the geometry itself was the failure, not the model.

High-confidence scores made it worse, not better. A segment misclassified at 0.9999 confidence is harder to catch than a low-confidence flag — it passes every threshold and surfaces nothing for review. The system wasn't degrading visibly; it was failing silently. The two JSON blocks below capture the contradiction: a long segment with near-certain confidence and a short one with zero — both wrong, both labeled Friedberg, both actually Brad.

The classifier was dropped. NeMo's speaker_id backbone and Streamlit HITL review replaced stochastic label assignment with something auditable. It also exposed me directly to working with transcripts at scale, revealing a new class of problems that a classifier score would have steamrolled over.
