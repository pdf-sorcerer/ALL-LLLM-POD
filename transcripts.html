Transcript Fidelity

Transcripts became a genuine hero’s journey. Early on, my mindset was to speedrun development by moving fast and running code for systems I didn’t yet fully understand.

There’s honestly a novel’s worth of failures, lessons learned, and cursed experiments buried inside this pipeline. Keeping things high-level, the final architecture evolved through several major iterations:

1. I replaced Pyannote with NVIDIA NeMo. NeMo was much more accurate identifying unique speakers, and produced tighter embeddings. picture // picture
2. NeMo’s RTTM diarization boundaries warped the transcript into fragmentation patterns like:

Mason: I
UNKNOWN: like
Mason: tacos

4. From there, I built lightweight HITL heuristics to identify suspicious transcript artifacts: ellipsis boundaries, lowercase continuations, dangling sentence terminators, speaker sandwiches, and other structural fingerprints that reliably surfaced hidden attribution failures.

WhisperX transcripts are word-level aligned to the millisecond, so I used them rather than rely on NeMo's RTTM data. Good decision.

Boundary Integrity Flags

Transcript failures often leave recognizable structural fingerprints. Over time, a small set of heuristic flags emerged that reliably surfaced suspicious segments for Human-in-the-Loop review.

1 — Ellipsis Boundary

A segment presents as one continuous speaker turn, but often contains two speakers (although this doesn't happen when Friedberg is speaking). Fingerprint: mid-segment ellipsis followed by conversational continuation.

Before
Sacks: “Interest rates just have to go up to a point that…”
Sacks: “To what's happening in Japan, right?”

After
Sacks: “Interest rates just have to go up to a point that…”
Jcal: “To what's happening in Japan, right?”

2 — Speaker Collapse Blob

A single speaker_id absorbs a long multi-speaker exchange. Fingerprint: unusually long monologue segments containing clearly distinct conversational voices.

3 — Backchannel Bleed

Short acknowledgements (“okay,” “yeah,” “got it”) become absorbed into the acoustically dominant nearby speaker because the embedding window is too short for reliable classification.

Before
Sacks: “...tell them I said hi too.”
Jcal: “Oh, okay.”
Sacks: “Got it.”

After
Sacks: “...tell them I said hi too.”
Jcal: “Oh, okay. Got it.”

4 — Lowercase Continuation

ASR systems often capitalize new conversational turns automatically. A lowercase opening strongly suggests the segment is actually a continuation from the prior speaker.

5 — Third-Person Self Reference

The attributed speaker refers to themselves by name inside their own segment — usually a strong signal the segment belongs to someone else.

6 — Speaker Sandwich

Three adjacent segments form an A→B→A pattern where the middle speaker never actually spoke. Fingerprint: middle segment ends in ellipsis, third segment resumes as a lowercase continuation.
