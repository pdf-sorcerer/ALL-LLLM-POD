Transcript Reconstruction

Ghost Audio

In February 2026, I switched from downloading episode audio from YouTube to downloading full episode masters directly from Libsyn. The change was mostly about convenience: one source, one workflow, fewer moving parts.

I ran a small batch of episodes through my ASR pipeline and compared the new v2 transcript of Episode 136 side-by-side with its original v1 counterpart. A short line from Friedberg — "Oh, my God" at roughly 20 seconds — was missing from the newer transcript.

For completeness, guest Brad Gerstner says nearly the same phrase a few seconds later. He's barely audible; I only noticed the utterance while reviewing the video. Gerstner's line was absent from both transcripts and was never recovered.

I coined this phenomenon Ghost Audio because Friedberg's speech was clearly audible, yet the missing text left almost no evidence it had ever existed.

The missing line itself isn't the problem. The problem is that omissions distort the surrounding conversation. Remove enough speech and speaker turns collapse, context disappears, and downstream training examples begin teaching the wrong relationships between prompts and completions.

Here's a relatable (if exaggerated) example of what that can look like:

Episode 136 · Both ghost regions · ~20–29s

00:17 Chamath: The shortest, that's the fattest. And the fattest, that's the dumbest.

00:21 Friedberg: Oh, my God. He's not even here. You can't do that. It's awful. That's just bullying. It's awful.

00:17 Chamath: The shortest, that's the fattest. And the fattest, that's the dumbest.

00:25 Friedberg: He's not even here. You can't do that. It's awful. That's just bullying. It's awful.

The audio was there. Spectrogram, RMS energy, and pitch detection all confirmed real speech in both windows. The pipeline knew speech existed there. It just didn't transcribe it.

Token chain analysis showed why the two cases are different. At Friedberg's decision point — immediately after the decoder emits the <|19.04|> timestamp — "Oh" ranked first at 86%. At Gerstner's, it ranked 134th at 0.004%. Same audio segment, same decoder, completely different probability landscape.

The Compound Failure

Isolating the fix took longer than expected because there's no single cause. Friedberg drops only when four conditions are true simultaneously. Break any one and he surfaces.

Necessary conditions for failure · ALL must be true
Condition Value Break it? Result
Model large-v3 Use large-v2 ✓ Recovered
Pipeline WhisperX Raw transformers ✓ Recovered
Timestamps without_timestamps=True Set False ✓ Recovered
Beam size beam_size ≥ 5 (default) Use beam=1 ✓ Recovered

One working fix is the setting without_timestamps=False, aka: 'timestamps are ON'. WhisperX with timestamps enabled recovers Friedberg at every beam size tested. Why exactly this combination fails — what beam search scores differently without timestamp tokens — isn't fully understood. Timestamp tokens are trained jointly with text in Whisper, not bolted on. Removing them changes how the decoder scores competing paths in non-obvious ways. CTranslate2 doesn't expose decoder internals for inspection.

Gerstner-Class Failure

Gerstner is a different problem. His "Oh" ranked 134th at 0.004% — the decoder never seriously considered it. What it did instead was collapse: looping "I'm bullying when you're bullying" three times over audio VAD confirmed as real speech. The hallucination wasn't over silence. It was over audio the decoder couldn't recover.

Repeated phrase plus wav2vec alignment failure over a VAD-positive region is the fingerprint for this class of ghost — unrecoverable audio the decoder fills rather than transcribes. Nothing tested recovered Gerstner. The fingerprint is detectable at corpus scale, which means every instance can be flagged even if the content can't be recovered. That turns an unknown unknown into a known gap.

Ghost Audio vs. Misclassification

If you're familiar with cinema in general, Star Wars should need no introduction nor spoiler alert. This scnene from the movie "Empire Strikes Back (1980)" exemplifies the impact ghost audio could have on LLMs that are ingesting transcripts as training data. In this scene, Luke learns Darth Vader is his father and reacts strongly to that. With ghot audio, Vader's relevation is never transcribed - and Luke's spoken content collapses. This does sseveral things for the model: it omits the father-son relationship Luke and Vader have, and it informs the model that Luke says things and immediately contracts himself.

Movie Transcript

Vader: "Obi-Wan never told you what happened to your father."

Luke: "He told me enough. He told me you killed him!"

Vader: "No. I am your father."

Luke: "No, no. That's not true. That's impossible!"

Ghost Audio Transcript

Vader: "Obi-Wan never told you what happened to your father."

Luke: "He told me enough. He told me you killed him! No, no. That's not true. That's impossible!"
