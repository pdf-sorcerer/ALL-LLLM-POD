Transcript Reconstruction

Ghost Audio

In early 2026, I migrated the pipeline to Libsyn, despite already having processed more than 560 episodes from YouTube. YouTube was a messy source of truth: full episodes coexisted with shorter-form video and content needed to be deduplicated. Episode numbering and naming conventions also drifted. Regardless, switching to Libsyn made all that a non-issue.

At time of writing (June 2026), there are: 383 Libsyn episodes · 384 Spotify episodes · 2,230 official YouTube uploads

Libsyn was intended to be a going-forward change, but I decided to re-run a handful of episodes—including Episode 136. While reviewing the episode, I noticed a short interjection from Friedberg around t≈20s: "Oh, my God." The phrase was present in a transcript generated in Fall 2025, but missing from the newer Libsyn transcript and therefore absent from the persona training corpus.

Moments later, guest Brad Gerstner says the exact same phrase. His line is missing from every transcript. I only know he said it because I happened to be watching the video while investigating Friedberg's omission.

I began calling this phenomenon Ghost Audio because Friedberg's speech is clearly audible, yet every attempt to reproduce it in my AI pipeline seemed to end the same way: extensive investigation, no explanation—not unlike paranormal investigation tv shows of the 1990s.

Up to this point, all AI errors I experienced in transcription left fingerprints: ellipses, unterminated segments, lowercase continuations, alignment failures, or other artifacts I discuss in the heuristics section. Omissions like Ghost Audio are different classs: they leave no fingerprints, but distort speaker turns, context, and relationships between ideas. Remove enough speech, dialogue collapses and the model is taught the wrong relationships between prompts and completions.

Vader: "Obi-Wan never told you what happened to your father."

Fans should point out that the scene also contains Vader: "Join me, and together we can rule the galaxy as father and son." True. I omitted it for brevity; ironic given the subject here. Even so, the example illustrates the core problem: transcript omissions do not just remove words. They remove context, causality, and relationships between ideas.

The audio was there. Spectrogram, RMS energy, and pitch detection all confirmed real speech, though pitch was recoverable only for Friedberg.

Token chain analysis showed why the two cases are different. At Friedberg's decision point — immediately after the decoder emits the <|19.04|> timestamp — "Oh" ranked first at 86%. At Gerstner's, it ranked 134th at 0.004%. Same audio segment, same decoder, completely different probability landscape.

The Compound Failure

Isolating the fix took longer than expected because there's no single cause. Friedberg drops only when four conditions are true simultaneously. Break any one and he surfaces.

Necessary conditions for failure · ALL must be true
Condition Value Break it? Result
Model large-v3 Use large-v2 ✓ Recovered
Pipeline WhisperX Raw transformers ✓ Recovered
Timestamps without_timestamps=True Set False ✓ Recovered
Beam size beam_size ≥ 5 (default) Use beam=1 ✓ Recovered

One working fix is the setting without_timestamps=False, aka: 'timestamps are ON'. WhisperX with timestamps enabled recovers Friedberg at every beam size tested. Why exactly this combination fails — what beam search scores differently without timestamp tokens — isn't fully understood. Timestamp tokens are trained jointly with text in Whisper, not bolted on. Removing them changes how the decoder scores competing paths in non-obvious ways. CTranslate2 doesn't expose decoder internals for inspection.

Gerstner-Class Failure

Gerstner is a different problem. His "Oh" ranked 134th at 0.004% — the decoder never seriously considered it. What it did instead was collapse: looping "I'm bullying when you're bullying" three times over audio VAD confirmed as real speech. The hallucination wasn't over silence. It was over audio the decoder couldn't recover.

Repeated phrase plus wav2vec alignment failure over a VAD-positive region is the fingerprint for this class of ghost — unrecoverable audio the decoder fills rather than transcribes. Nothing tested recovered Gerstner. The fingerprint is detectable at corpus scale, which means every instance can be flagged even if the content can't be recovered. That turns an unknown unknown into a known gap.
