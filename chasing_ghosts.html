Transcript Reconstruction

Ghost Audio

In early 2026, I migrated the pipeline to Libsyn, despite already having processed more than 560 episodes from YouTube. YouTube was a messy source of truth: full episodes coexisted with shorter-form video and content needed to be deduplicated. Episode numbering and naming conventions also drifted. Regardless, switching to Libsyn made all that a non-issue.

At time of writing (June 2026), there are: 383 Libsyn episodes · 384 Spotify episodes · 2,230 official YouTube uploads

Libsyn was intended to be a going-forward change, but I decided to re-run a handful of episodes—including Episode 136. While reviewing the episode, I noticed a short interjection from Friedberg around t≈20s: "Oh, my God." The phrase was present in a transcript generated in Fall 2025, but missing from the newer Libsyn transcript and therefore absent from the persona training corpus.

Moments later, guest Brad Gerstner says the exact same phrase. His line is missing from every transcript. I only know he said it because I happened to be watching the video while investigating Friedberg's omission.

I began calling this phenomenon Ghost Audio because Friedberg's speech is clearly audible, yet every attempt to reproduce it in my AI pipeline seemed to end the same way: extensive investigation, no explanation.

Up to this point, all AI errors I experienced left fingerprints discussed in Heuristics. Omissions like Ghost Audio examples distort speaker turns, context, and relationships between ideas and leave no detectable fingerprints. Remove enough speech and dialogue collapses. Downstream training examples begin teaching the wrong relationships between prompts and completions.

YouTube ASR failed to transcribe a single-word response from Batman: "Them?". That response communicates a sudden shift in Batman's understanding: Joker has two hostages, not one.

The transcript still reads cleanly. Nothing appears broken. Yet the moment of realization—and the causal link between Joker's statement and Batman's reaction—disappears.

The audio was there. Spectrogram, RMS energy, and pitch detection all confirmed real speech. The transcript was the part that failed.

Decoder Fork

Friedberg raised a simple question: what was the decoder actually seeing?

The production transcript omitted his "Oh, my God.", yet small changes to the decoding strategy could bring it back. Rather than treating Whisper as a black box, I built a separate PyTorch decoder pipeline to inspect the token probabilities directly.

The results showed the missing utterance was not impossible. At Friedberg's decision point, immediately after <|19.04|>, the decoder had two viable futures. One path matched the audio. The other scored higher.

The transformer was not choosing between truth and falsehood. It was choosing between two beam paths. The acoustically correct path lost by 5.75 percentage points.

That observation changed the investigation. If losing beam paths could sometimes represent reality, then failed decoding attempts were not just noise. They were evidence.

BERD-1

BERD-1 grew out of that fork. Instead of asking one production pipeline for the truth, I compared multiple decoder strategies against the same audio and looked for disagreement.

Some of the alternate runs produced lower-quality transcripts. Some produced repetitions. Some hallucinated. Under normal transcript generation, those outputs would be discarded. In BERD-1, they became probes.

Stable regions produced stable transcripts. Unstable regions did not. When the production transcript, greedy PyTorch decode, beam-search probes, and alignment checks disagreed over the same short VAD-positive segment, the disagreement became a review candidate.

The goal was never to trust the hallucinations. The goal was to use them to find places where the decoder had another story to tell.
